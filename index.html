Ferrous Metals
Iron-based · ~90% of all metals used globally
Any metal with iron (Fe) as its primary ingredient. The backbone of construction, automotive manufacturing, and industrial infrastructure worldwide.
Steel (HRC/CRC) Tinplate Galvanized Cast Iron Stainless Steel Alloy Steel
Key driver: Iron ore and coking coal costs set the floor. The carbon content in the iron-carbon mix determines hardness vs. ductility. Pure iron is soft — add carbon to get steel, more carbon to get cast iron. Ferrous metals rust, which is why coatings (tinplate, galvanized) exist — and why they trade at a premium to HRC.

Non-Ferrous Metals
No iron · Lightweight · Corrosion-resistant
Metals that contain no significant iron content. Generally lighter, non-magnetic, and naturally resistant to corrosion. Critical for packaging, electronics, transport, and wire.
Aluminum (Al) Copper (Cu) Zinc (Zn) Tin (Sn) Lead (Pb) Nickel (Ni)
Directly linked to our work: Tin coats steel to make tinplate. Zinc coats steel to make galvanized. Both are traded on the LME and their prices directly feed into our downstream steel product pricing. This is the bridge between ferrous and non-ferrous markets.

✦
Precious Metals
Rare · High unit value · Store of wealth
Scarce metals with high economic value per unit. Used in jewellery, as financial instruments, in electronics, and as catalysts in auto and chemical industries.
Gold (Au) Silver (Ag) Platinum (Pt) Palladium (Pd) Rhodium (Rh)
Priced in troy oz (Gold, Silver, Pt) or $/oz. Platinum group metals (PGMs) are critical for automotive catalytic converters. Gold is macro-sensitive — moves on USD, interest rates, and geopolitical risk. Very different demand dynamics from industrial metals.

⚡
Battery Materials
EV-critical · Energy transition · Fast-growing
Metals powering the energy transition. Demand tightly linked to electric vehicle production, energy storage systems, and consumer electronics.
Lithium (Li) Cobalt (Co) Nickel (Ni) Manganese (Mn) Graphite (C)
Supply is geographically concentrated and politically sensitive — DRC (cobalt), Chile/Australia (lithium). Prices highly volatile, driven by EV adoption forecasts, Chinese policy, and mine supply disruptions. Fastest-growing segment in metals analysis.

HRC — Hot Rolled Coil
The global ferrous benchmark. Every flat steel product price is built on top of HRC. Produced directly from a reheated slab through a hot rolling mill at ~1,200°C. The rough, scaled surface is characteristic — it's why you need further processing for premium applications.
Thickness: 1.5–12mm   |   Surface: Mill scale (rough)   |   Uses: Construction, structural, pipes, auto frames   |   Quoted on: CME, SGX, Platts, Fastmarkets, SHFE
Module 4 · ~8 min
The Price Detective: Deriving Tinplate
Tinplate prices are not publicly published anywhere. But you can build them from first principles — using just HRC and LME Tin prices. This is exactly how we do it.
The Core Formula
Tinplate Price  =  CRC Price  +  Tin Metal Cost  +  Electrolytic Processing Premium
where   CRC = HRC + cold rolling premium   |   Tin Metal Cost = LME Tin ($/t) × coating weight (kg per tonne of steel) ÷ 1000
1
Get the HRC price
Source from Fastmarkets, Platts (S&P Global), or exchange data (CME, SGX, SHFE). Use the relevant regional benchmark — European HRC, US Midwest HRC, or Asian (CFR SE Asia) depending on your geography.
2
Add the cold rolling premium → CRC
Cold rolling thins and smooths the strip. Then it's annealed to restore ductility. This adds $80–120/t over HRC. The spread varies with mill utilisation and regional supply/demand. CRC is also not always directly published — you may also need to derive this.
3
Get LME Tin price and calculate tin metal cost
Tin is traded on the London Metal Exchange (LME) in $/tonne. Tinplate uses a thin coating of tin on both sides — typically 2.8 to 8.4 g/m² per side depending on grade. A standard T57 grade (2.8/2.8 g/m²) uses ~3.2–3.4 kg of tin per tonne of steel. Multiply by LME Tin price (converted to $/kg) to get the tin metal cost component.
4
Add the electrolytic tinning processing premium
The electrolytic tinning line (ETL) applies tin in a controlled electroplating process. This adds $130–160/t for standard grades. Premium grades, narrow widths, or special surface finishes attract higher processing fees from mills.
5
Cross-check with mill offers and market intelligence
The derived price is your model estimate. Cross-check with mill spot offers (where available), industry contacts, and trade publications. Actual traded prices may deviate due to contract terms, regional logistics, and supply tightness. Your estimate provides the baseline for negotiation and analysis.
