The Metal Universe
Metals trade across four distinct families — each with its own feedstocks, pricing benchmarks, and market dynamics. Click each card to dig deeper.
⚙️
Ferrous Metals
Iron-based · ~90% of all metals used globally
Any metal with iron (Fe) as its primary ingredient. The backbone of construction, automotive manufacturing, and industrial infrastructure worldwide.
Steel (HRC/CRC) Tinplate Galvanized Cast Iron Stainless Steel Alloy Steel
Key driver: Iron ore and coking coal costs set the floor. The carbon content in the iron-carbon mix determines hardness vs. ductility. Pure iron is soft — add carbon to get steel, more carbon to get cast iron. Ferrous metals rust, which is why coatings (tinplate, galvanized) exist — and why they trade at a premium to HRC.

Non-Ferrous Metals
No iron · Lightweight · Corrosion-resistant
Metals that contain no significant iron content. Generally lighter, non-magnetic, and naturally resistant to corrosion. Critical for packaging, electronics, transport, and wire.
Aluminum (Al) Copper (Cu) Zinc (Zn) Tin (Sn) Lead (Pb) Nickel (Ni)
Directly linked to our work: Tin coats steel to make tinplate. Zinc coats steel to make galvanized. Both are traded on the LME and their prices directly feed into our downstream steel product pricing. This is the bridge between ferrous and non-ferrous markets.

✦
Precious Metals
Rare · High unit value · Store of wealth
Scarce metals with high economic value per unit. Used in jewellery, as financial instruments, in electronics, and as catalysts in auto and chemical industries.
Gold (Au) Silver (Ag) Platinum (Pt) Palladium (Pd) Rhodium (Rh)
Priced in troy oz (Gold, Silver, Pt) or $/oz. Platinum group metals (PGMs) are critical for automotive catalytic converters. Gold is macro-sensitive — moves on USD, interest rates, and geopolitical risk. Very different demand dynamics from industrial metals.

Battery Materials
EV-critical · Energy transition · Fast-growing
Metals powering the energy transition. Demand tightly linked to electric vehicle production, energy storage systems, and consumer electronics.
Lithium (Li) Cobalt (Co) Nickel (Ni) Manganese (Mn) Graphite (C)
Supply is geographically concentrated and politically sensitive — DRC (cobalt), Chile/Australia (lithium). Prices highly volatile, driven by EV adoption forecasts, Chinese policy, and mine supply disruptions. Fastest-growing segment in metals analysis.

Module 2 · ~8 min
How Steel is Made
9 stages, two production routes — click any numbered hotspot to explore each step. Both BF-BOF (amber) and EAF (blue) routes are shown simultaneously, converging at refining.
01 Input raw materials BF-BOF + EAF
The steelmaking process feeds on two distinct input streams. The BF-BOF route needs iron ore, coking coal, and limestone — mined globally and shipped to coastal mills. The EAF route primarily uses scrap steel plus DRI/HBI for higher grades.
Inputs
  • Iron ore (Fe₂O₃ / Fe₃O₄)
  • Coking coal (metallurgical)
  • Limestone (CaCO₃)
  • Scrap steel (HMS 1&2, shredded)
  • Natural gas / hydrogen (DRI)
Key facts
  • ~70% BF-BOF globally
  • ~30% EAF globally
  • Australia/Brazil supply iron ore
  • ~1.9B tonnes steel/year
~70% BF-BOF globally ~30% EAF globally Australia/Brazil — iron ore 1.9B tonnes/yr global

1
Raw Material Inputs
Three feedstocks drive everything: iron ore, coking coal, and limestone — mined globally and converged at coastal steel mills.
Iron Ore (Fe₂O₃) provides the iron. Mainly mined in Australia, Brazil, and South Africa. Traded as lump, fines, and pellets.

Coking Coal (Metallurgical Coal) is converted to coke — the reducing agent and heat source. Different from thermal coal used in power stations.

Limestone (CaCO₃) acts as a flux to capture impurities as slag, which floats on the molten iron.
Iron OreCoking CoalLimestoneKey feedstocks
2
Preparation — Coking & Sintering
Coal is baked into coke. Fine iron ore fines are sintered into lumpy pellets that can survive the blast furnace.
Coke Ovens: Coal heated to ~1,000°C without air. Drives off volatile compounds. Output: porous, carbon-rich coke with strength to withstand the blast furnace without crumbling.

Sintering Plant: Fine iron ore (too small to charge directly) mixed with coke breeze and flux, then ignited. Agglomerates into porous sinter for the furnace. Improves furnace productivity by 15–20%.
Coke Ovens ~1,000°CSintering PlantPelletizing
3
Blast Furnace — Ironmaking
Charged from the top with coke, sinter, and limestone. Hot air blasted in at the base. Molten pig iron tapped from the bottom every few hours.
The blast furnace is the heart of an integrated steelmill — typically 30–100m tall. Temperatures reach ~2,000°C at the tuyeres (air inlets). Coke burns, produces CO gas which reduces iron oxides to metallic iron. Limestone flux combines with silica impurities to form slag (lower density, floats on top of iron).

Output = Pig Iron (~4% carbon) — very brittle, cannot be used directly. Must be refined.
~2,000°CPig Iron output~4% CarbonSlag by-product
4
Basic Oxygen Furnace (BOF) — Steelmaking
Pig iron + scrap charged into a converter. Pure oxygen blown in at supersonic speed burns off excess carbon. Steel ready in ~40 minutes.
A supersonic oxygen lance oxidises excess carbon (4% → <0.1%). This exothermic reaction reaches ~1,700°C. 20–30% scrap is added as a coolant. Alloying elements (Mn, Si, Al) added to achieve target grade composition. This is where iron becomes steel — workable, ductile, weldable.
Oxygen Lance20–30% Scrap<0.1% Carbon~40 min cycle
5
Continuous Casting → Slab
Liquid steel poured into a water-cooled mould and drawn out continuously. Solidifies into slabs — the universal semi-finished flat product.
Slabs are 200–250mm thick, 900–2,000mm wide. This is the input for ALL flat products downstream: HRC, CRC, galvanized, tinplate. Steel also cast as billets (long products: rebar, wire rod) or blooms (structural shapes). The slab is where the two routes converge — EAF makes the same slab.
Slab ~200mm thickFlat productsBillets → long products
6
Hot Rolling Mill → HRC 🎯
Slabs reheated to ~1,200°C and squeezed through a series of rolling stands. Emerges as Hot Rolled Coil (HRC) — the global ferrous benchmark.
HRC is the key price anchor. Everything downstream references it. Typical HRC: 1.5–12mm thick, width up to 2,000mm, coiled for transport. Surface has characteristic mill scale (dark oxide layer) — rough texture. Used directly in construction, structural fabrication, pipe welding, and as the input for further processing into CRC/galvanized/tinplate.
HRC 1.5–12mm~1,200°C rollingGlobal benchmarkCME / SGX / Platts
1
Scrap Steel & DRI Inputs
Old cars, demolished buildings, appliance scrap — collected, sorted by grade, and charged directly. The primary feedstock, no blast furnace needed.
Scrap grades matter: HMS 1&2 (Heavy Melting Scrap), shredded auto scrap, prime industrial scrap. Quality of scrap determines quality of steel output — limiting factor for flat product grades.

DRI/HBI (Direct Reduced Iron) — iron ore reduced with natural gas or hydrogen, not coal. A cleaner substitute that upgrades EAF output quality. Key to green steel pathways.
HMS 1&2 ScrapShredded ScrapDRI / HBINo iron ore needed
2
Electric Arc Furnace (EAF)
Three graphite electrodes generate an arc at up to 3,000–4,000°C, melting scrap in 60–90 minutes. No coke, no blast furnace — just electricity.
EAF is faster and more flexible than BF-BOF. Can start and stop production quickly — useful for responding to demand. Key cost driver: electricity price. Where power is cheap (Turkey, India, parts of US), EAF economics are very competitive.

CO₂ footprint ~30–40% of BF-BOF when powered by grid electricity. With renewable power or H₂-DRI, can approach near-zero emissions.
Graphite Electrodes60–90 min tap-to-tapLower CO₂~30% global share
3
Ladle Refining & Continuous Casting
Chemistry fine-tuned in a ladle furnace. Then continuously cast into slabs — same output as BF-BOF from this point on.
From the slab onwards, the process is identical to BF-BOF. Hot rolling → HRC. Cold rolling → CRC. Coating → galvanized / tinplate. EAF route represents ~30% of global steel production today and is growing rapidly, especially in regions transitioning to green steel.

Module 4 · ~8 min
The Price Detective: Deriving Tinplate
Tinplate prices are not publicly published anywhere. But you can build them from first principles — using just HRC and LME Tin prices. This is exactly how we do it.
The Core Formula
Tinplate Price  =  CRC Price  +  Tin Metal Cost  +  Electrolytic Processing Premium
where   CRC = HRC + cold rolling premium   |   Tin Metal Cost = LME Tin ($/t) × coating weight (kg per tonne of steel) ÷ 1000
1
Get the HRC price
Source from Fastmarkets, Platts (S&P Global), or exchange data (CME, SGX, SHFE). Use the relevant regional benchmark — European HRC, US Midwest HRC, or Asian (CFR SE Asia) depending on your geography.
2
Add the cold rolling premium → CRC
Cold rolling thins and smooths the strip. Then it's annealed to restore ductility. This adds $80–120/t over HRC. The spread varies with mill utilisation and regional supply/demand. CRC is also not always directly published — you may also need to derive this.
3
Get LME Tin price and calculate tin metal cost
Tin is traded on the London Metal Exchange (LME) in $/tonne. Tinplate uses a thin coating of tin on both sides — typically 2.8 to 8.4 g/m² per side depending on grade. A standard T57 grade (2.8/2.8 g/m²) uses ~3.2–3.4 kg of tin per tonne of steel. Multiply by LME Tin price (converted to $/kg) to get the tin metal cost component.
4
Add the electrolytic tinning processing premium
The electrolytic tinning line (ETL) applies tin in a controlled electroplating process. This adds $130–160/t for standard grades. Premium grades, narrow widths, or special surface finishes attract higher processing fees from mills.
5
Cross-check with mill offers and market intelligence
The derived price is your model estimate. Cross-check with mill spot offers (where available), industry contacts, and trade publications. Actual traded prices may deviate due to contract terms, regional logistics, and supply tightness. Your estimate provides the baseline for negotiation and analysis.
